Turning 150,000+ customer records into behavior-based product recommendations

HackUTD best-in-show. Analyzed 150,000+ customer records to identify behavior-based product recommendation opportunities, defined the product logic, built user flows, and presented design concepts judged best-in-show by industry judges.

Most product recommendation systems use demographics. We used behavior. The insight driving the project: customers who look the same on paper (similar income, similar account type) behave very differently. If you segment by what they actually do rather than who they are, the right product recommendation becomes obvious. We analyzed 150,000+ customer records to prove it, then built the product logic and experience around the finding.

30-second version

Financial institutions sit on enormous customer data but surface product recommendations that don't reflect what customers actually do. We analyzed 150,000+ customer records, segmented by behavioral patterns rather than demographics, and identified where the mismatch between customer behavior and current product offerings was largest. From there we defined the recommendation logic, built the user flows, and presented the product concept. Industry judges awarded best in show.

01: Context

Context

Financial institutions have more customer data than they know what to do with. The problem is not data availability. The problem is that most product recommendation systems are built on demographic proxies: account type, income bracket, age. Those signals are easy to collect and easy to segment on, but they are poor predictors of what a customer actually needs.

Two customers with similar profiles (same income tier, same account age, same product set) can behave completely differently. One runs a surplus every month and ignores their savings account. The other carries a recurring balance and has never been shown a lower-rate product. Demographics do not distinguish them. Behavior does.

03: Problem

The problem with demographic segmentation

Most financial product recommendation is built on demographic segmentation: age, income, account tenure. Those proxies are easy to compute and easy to explain in a product spec. The problem is that they do not predict product fit. Two customers with nearly identical demographic profiles can have completely different needs based on how they actually use their accounts.

The product question we started with: if we segment 150,000+ customer records by what customers do rather than who they are, do we find a better signal for product recommendations? The answer turned out to be yes, and the segments were meaningfully distinct.

Three constraints drove every decision:

  • Behavioral signal over demographics. Segment by actual transaction patterns, not account type or income bracket.
  • Product logic has to follow the segment. Different behavioral profiles have different optimal product recommendations. The logic has to be segment-specific, not one-size-fits-all.
  • The experience has to surface the right recommendation at the right moment. A correct recommendation shown at the wrong time is ignored. Timing is part of the product logic.
04: Analysis

Behavioral segmentation

We ran K-means clustering across 150,000+ customer records, using transaction-level signals: spending volatility, category distribution, savings-to-spending ratio, and frequency patterns. Demographic variables were excluded intentionally. The goal was to see whether behavior alone produced meaningful, stable segments.

The clustering produced four distinct segments:

  • Consistent budgeters: low volatility, predictable category distribution, active savings behavior
  • Variable spenders: high month-to-month volatility, lifestyle-skewed categories, low savings buffer
  • Goal-driven savers: high savings rate, constrained discretionary spending, strong category discipline
  • Income-volatile users: irregular income patterns, reactive spending, high timing sensitivity

Each segment had a different optimal product recommendation. Consistent budgeters had unused savings product capacity. Variable spenders showed patterns consistent with needing a short-term buffer product. Goal-driven savers were candidates for higher-yield savings options. Income-volatile users needed timing-aware credit products more than any other segment. Demographic segmentation would have grouped most of these customers together.

05: Product logic

Recommendation logic

With the behavioral segments defined, the product logic had two jobs: match each customer to the right product recommendation for their segment, and surface that recommendation at the right moment in the experience.

Recommendation mapping: Each segment had a primary and secondary product recommendation based on the behavioral patterns identified in the analysis. The logic was explicit rather than probabilistic: if a customer's transaction behavior consistently matches the consistent-budgeter profile, they are a candidate for savings-rate optimization products. If they match the variable-spender profile, the priority recommendation shifts to a short-term buffer product. Segment-to-product mapping was defined before any interface work started.

Timing logic: A recommendation shown at the wrong moment is ignored. We defined trigger conditions for each segment that would surface the recommendation when the behavioral signal was strongest. For income-volatile customers, that meant surfacing a buffer product recommendation in the window after income deposits. For variable spenders, it meant surfacing a spending-pattern alert when category spend crossed a threshold relative to their own baseline, not a generic benchmark.

The user flows were designed around these two logic layers. The product recommendation was never surfaced as a generic prompt. It appeared when the customer's own behavior indicated they were in the window where the recommendation was most likely to land.

06: Design decisions

Key design decisions

Three decisions that shaped the product and required tradeoffs under time pressure.

Core decision

Segment before you advise

We could have built a simpler system: one prompt template, no clustering, generic context window. It would have been faster to build and easier to demo. We chose not to because the guidance it produced was obviously generic. Usable in a proof-of-concept, but not believable as a real product. The segmentation layer added implementation complexity but made every output feel like it was about the specific user. That was the difference the judges noticed.

Core decision

Behavioral segmentation over demographic proxies

Most recommendation systems in fintech use income, age, and account type as proxies for product fit. These are easy to compute but poor predictors of what a customer actually needs. We made the decision early to exclude demographic variables entirely from the clustering and test whether behavioral signals alone produced stable, meaningful segments. They did. The four segments we identified had distinct product recommendation profiles that demographic groupings would have collapsed into one or two buckets. That distinction became the core of the product pitch.

Core decision

Segment-specific recommendations over a single model

We could have built a single recommendation engine: one model, one product, shown to all users. It would have been faster to build and easier to explain. We chose to make the recommendation logic segment-specific because the segments had genuinely different needs. Showing a higher-yield savings product to an income-volatile customer who needs a credit buffer is not just unhelpful; it's a signal that the system does not understand the customer. The judges specifically noted that the segmentation made the recommendations feel credible rather than generic.

08: Measurement

How I would measure success

If Promising Products moved beyond prototype into a real financial product recommendation system, I would track the following:

North star metric Recommendation adoption rate: the percentage of surfaced product recommendations that result in a customer inquiring about or opening the recommended product within 30 days. This indicates the segmentation is producing recommendations with real commercial relevance, not just technically accurate cluster assignments.

Supporting KPIs:

  • Recommendation click-through rate by segment: whether engagement rates vary meaningfully across segments. If all four segments convert at similar rates, the segmentation is not doing real work.
  • Segment stability: how often customers shift segment assignments month-over-month. Excessive movement indicates noise in the clustering; no movement indicates the segments may be too coarse to capture real behavioral change.
  • Timing conversion lift: whether recommendations surfaced during the defined behavioral window (shortly after an income deposit, after a threshold crossing) convert at higher rates than the same recommendation shown at a random time. Confirms timing logic is adding value.

Counter-metric: Recommendation dismissal rate within the first session. A high rate signals the segment-to-product mapping is mis-calibrated: the right segment is being identified but the product recommendation is not matching the need.

09: Reflection

Reflection

The best decision we made was the one that cost us the most time: committing to behavioral segmentation before we started building anything else. It would have been faster to skip it. The AI responses without it were technically functional but obviously generic. Segmentation made the difference between a demo that impressed and a demo that raised product questions. That is a better kind of problem to have.

The thing I would do differently: test the segment-to-product recommendation mapping with real users before the demo rather than deriving it from the data alone. We defined the mapping based on what the behavioral patterns implied. That logic held up under judging, but it was an untested assumption carrying significant product weight. In a real product development cycle, you would want a round of qualitative research validating that the segment profiles match how customers actually understand their own financial situation before committing to a recommendation mapping.

The broader product lesson from this project: the hardest design problem in recommendation systems is not the interface. The interface is downstream. The relevance problem lives in how you model the user. What you cluster on, what features you weight, whether your segments are stable. Get that wrong and no amount of interface polish makes the output land. Get it right and a relatively simple interface can feel like a genuinely useful tool. The segmentation work was where this project was won or lost, not the prototype.
