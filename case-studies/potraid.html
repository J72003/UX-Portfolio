Potr-Aid — translating an AI verdict
into something a person can act on

An honors research project on portrait composition. A published paper, a 92% accurate model, and the design problem at the heart of every confidence-scored interface.

Role Researcher · UX Designer · ML Contributor
Duration Spring 2024 — Fall 2024
Methods Dataset curation · CNN training · Failure-mode review · Prototype feedback
Output Published paper · Working prototype

01 Context

Potr-Aid started as an honors thesis question: can a machine learning model evaluate portrait photograph composition and give a photographer actionable feedback? The prior work on computational aesthetics treated image quality as a scalar score — a number that told you whether a photo was good but not why, and not what to change.

I worked as part of a small research team. My contributions spanned three areas: dataset curation and labeling, training and evaluating the CNN model, and designing the interface that made the model's output useful to a human photographer. The paper was submitted and accepted. The model achieved 92% classification accuracy on a held-out test set.

The design problem was the most interesting part: a 92% accurate model is not 100% accurate. Every result carries a confidence score. How do you surface that score to a non-technical user in a way that supports good decisions rather than blind trust — or blind rejection?

02 Role

This was a year-long honors research project on AI evaluation under faculty advisement. I ran the evaluation methodology — quantitative model performance analysis combined with qualitative failure-mode review — and translated findings into design hypotheses for the prediction interface.

03 Research and model work

Portrait composition quality is subjective enough to be a research problem in itself. I curated a dataset of labeled portrait photographs, coding each image across five compositional attributes: subject framing, rule-of-thirds alignment, background separation, eye contact, and exposure balance. Each attribute was binary — present or absent — so the labeling was defensible rather than aesthetic.

The CNN was trained on this labeled dataset using transfer learning from a pretrained image backbone. After training, the model output a per-image classification (well-composed / needs improvement) plus a confidence score and — via gradient-weighted class activation mapping — an attention overlay showing which regions of the image most influenced the verdict.

The attention overlay was the piece that made the design problem interesting. It gave us something specific to show the user: not just "this photo needs improvement" but "the model is focusing on this region, and here's what it found there."

SYSTEM FLOW · UPLOAD TO FEEDBACK

04 Design problem

The model had three outputs: a binary verdict (well-composed / needs improvement), a confidence score (0–100%), and an attention overlay (a heatmap showing which pixels most influenced the classification). The question was how to present these three things together without creating a misleading, confusing, or over-engineered interface.

Two failure modes I wanted to avoid:

  • Overtrust. If the verdict is displayed prominently with no confidence qualifier, users treat a 61% call as authoritative. A portrait is "bad" because the AI said so, even though the model barely cleared the decision boundary.
  • Undertrust. If the confidence score is displayed raw and foregrounded, users dismiss every sub-90% result. The model stops being useful because users have learned to ignore it.

The design had to thread between these: surface enough uncertainty to prevent blind trust, without so much uncertainty that the result became useless.

UPLOAD SCREEN · WIREFRAME

05 Trust calibration hypotheses

The interface direction shifted from showing a raw AI score to supporting calibrated trust through confidence language, attention overlays, and disclosure notes. The result interface rests on three hypotheses, derived directly from the failure-mode research. Each is a testable bet about how interface treatment shapes calibrated trust — and each could be validated through follow-on research with real photographers.

The result screen was organized around a hierarchy of certainty. The verdict (well-composed / needs improvement) was displayed at the top, but in a softer visual weight than I might otherwise use — set in the muted palette, not as a large proclamation. The confidence score sat immediately below it, with a plain-language gloss: "The model is fairly confident" at 80%+ and "The model is uncertain" below 60%.

The attention overlay was placed directly on the uploaded photo, using a heatmap-style highlight (warm tones over areas of high influence). This grounded the abstract score in something concrete — a specific region the user could look at and evaluate for themselves.

Below the overlay, a short composition note explained the finding in plain language. Not "gradient activation map indicates poor background separation" but "The model flagged the background region. Check whether the subject is visually separated from what's behind them." The language was written to be actionable without being prescriptive.

Critically: the interface included a persistent disclosure note at the bottom of every result — "This model is 92% accurate on the test set. Treat this as a second opinion, not a verdict." This was the most explicit trust-calibration mechanism in the design. It was also the one I had to argue for against the instinct to make the interface feel more polished and confident.

RESULT SCREEN · WITH ATTENTION OVERLAY

06 Reflection

The disclosure note was the decision I'm most confident about in hindsight. It felt slightly over-cautious at the time — we had a strong model, and making the uncertainty explicit risked undermining the product. But the opposite risk was worse: a photographer adjusting their style based on a borderline classification, because the interface made the model's judgment look more reliable than it was.

The attention overlay worked better than I expected as an explanatory tool. Participants who struggled to understand a verdict ("but the photo looks fine to me") changed their reasoning when they could see which region had triggered the classification. The overlay converted an abstract score into a spatial argument — something you could agree or disagree with, rather than just accept.

The deeper takeaway, which connects to the Inverte-Quest work: showing a confidence score is not the same as communicating uncertainty. A number requires numeracy and a reference point to interpret. What users actually need is an answer to "should I act on this, and how?" The score is an input to that answer, not the answer itself. The interface's job is to close that gap.
