Defining requirements for an AI-assisted field identification tool

Requirements definition for an NSF-funded mobile identification app. The core product problem was not "AI identifies organisms." It was: how do students, teachers, and field researchers capture usable data, interpret AI confidence, and save credible observations under real field constraints?

Role Product Researcher · Requirements Lead
Duration Fall 2024–Spring 2025
Methods Persona mapping · Workflow analysis · Competitive audit · Requirements definition · Prototype testing · AI confidence analysis
Tools Figma · Miro
30-second version

I defined requirements for an NSF-funded AI-assisted identification tool used by students, teachers, and field researchers. The core product challenge was not "identify organisms." It was helping users capture usable inputs, interpret uncertain predictions, and save credible observations in real field conditions. I mapped four user types (including the AI system itself), conducted prototype testing, and revised the low-confidence save flow after observing a requirements gap in user testing.

Product question

How should an AI-assisted field identification tool help users capture reliable inputs, interpret uncertain predictions, and save credible observations, in conditions where standard mobile UI patterns break down?

01 Context

Inverte-Quest is a concept mobile app developed as part of an NSF S-STEM research grant at Texas A&M University–San Antonio. The goal: give biology students and citizen scientists a way to photograph and identify aquatic macroinvertebrates in the field, using machine learning to surface a species match and a confidence score.

I came onto the project as UX designer and was handed two constraints that defined everything downstream. First: the app would be used in streams and creek banks: wet hands, bright sun, time pressure, no stable surface. Second: the ML model had meaningful accuracy variance across species. A high-confidence match on a common stonefly and a low-confidence guess on an obscure midge larva needed to feel different to the user.

01.5: User needs

Who uses this, and what they need

Four user types shaped every requirement. Three were people; one was the AI system itself, which has its own input quality requirement the product must satisfy.

User group Primary need Product requirement
Students Learn what they found without being overwhelmed Plain-language results, visual comparison, simple confidence labels
Teachers Review and organize class observations Saved logs, session grouping, low-friction review mode
Field researchers Record credible observations under imperfect conditions Metadata capture, confidence display, uncertainty framing, exportable notes
AI system Needs a usable image to make a reliable prediction Capture guidance, retake prompts, quality checks before submission

02 Evidence and research

I started with a competitive audit of iNaturalist, Seek, and several academic species ID tools. The pattern was consistent: most apps were designed for desktop-origin workflows (upload a photo you already have, review results later). The capture flow was treated as a formality: tap, confirm, done. Nothing accounted for the fact that capture itself is the hard part when you're standing in a stream.

I ran two field observation sessions watching biology students collect samples. Three findings dominated:

  • Students held their phone one-handed, meaning most UI interactions happened in a 2.5-inch thumb zone at the bottom of the screen.
  • Glare made standard dark overlays and small text nearly unreadable in direct sun. Students squinted and leaned in. Clear friction signal.
  • When a result showed low confidence, students didn't know whether to trust it, skip it, or photograph again. The confidence number was meaningless without a frame for what it implied.

03 The product problems

Two product problems sat under the surface of "build an ID app":

Capture quality is the first bottleneck

Most identification tools assume the user already has a clean image. In field conditions, capture quality is the product problem. Students stand in streams with one dry hand, deal with direct sunlight, and have seconds before the specimen moves. Standard mobile UI patterns break down here. The capture trigger had to live in the thumb zone, guidance had to be legible in bright sun, and the confirmation step had to be forgiving of accidental taps.

Product decision informed: One large shutter button in the thumb zone, minimal controls, framing guide visible in direct sun, no mid-capture menu.

AI uncertainty must be legible and actionable

The model had meaningful accuracy variance across species. A raw confidence percentage (72%) communicates nothing about what action to take. Users needed a result screen that answered "should I trust this?" and "what should I do next?" Not just "here is a prediction."

Product decision informed: Three confidence bands (high, medium, low) each with distinct visual treatment and a distinct call to action. Plain-language gloss alongside every score.

05 Product decisions

The capture screen resolved around a single principle: reduce the number of decisions required in the moment of capture. One large shutter button. A minimal viewfinder overlay showing only the framing guidance. No menu, no settings, no mode switching accessible mid-capture.

For the result screen, I separated confidence into three bands (high, medium, low), each with a distinct visual treatment and a distinct call to action. High confidence showed a bold match name and a single "Save" action. Medium confidence showed the match with a secondary "Recapture" option. Low confidence surfaced the top two candidates as equally weighted options, removed the default "Save" action, and replaced it with "Save as unidentified" and a note explaining why.

I also added a brief plain-language explanation under the confidence indicator. Instead of "Confidence: 61%" it read "The model found a close match. Photographing from a different angle may produce a clearer result." This closed the loop between the score and the action.

07 Prototype testing

I ran prototype testing with four biology students across two sessions. The capture flow tested well. Students found the shutter button without instruction and didn't comment on it, which was the intended outcome. The framing guide corners were described by one student as "feeling like a camera" rather than "feeling like an app," which tracked with the mental model I was designing toward.

The confidence result screens produced more feedback. The three-tier system worked in the high-confidence condition. In the low-confidence condition, two students initially tried to tap a candidate card to "select" it before saving, an interaction the prototype didn't support. This pointed to a gap: the candidates needed to be explicitly selectable, not just informational. I revised the design to add a radio selection state to each candidate card, which resolved the confusion in the follow-up session.

Prototype testing revealed that low-confidence candidate cards needed explicit selection states. That finding directly changed the save flow before any further iteration.

09 How I would measure success

North star metric Credible observation completion rate: the percentage of field sessions where a user captures, interprets, and saves an observation with enough confidence and metadata to be scientifically useful. This measures whether the product is actually helping users generate reliable data, not just identifying organisms.

Supporting KPIs:

  • Retake rate on low-confidence results: do users act on the retake prompt? A low rate signals the low-confidence treatment is being ignored.
  • Candidate selection rate vs. save-as-unidentified rate: indicates whether users are making intentional identification decisions or defaulting to a save action.
  • Save-anyway rate on low-confidence: users who skip the retake and save a low-confidence prediction without selection. This is the key counter-signal for trust calibration failure.
  • Teacher review completion rate: whether teachers are actually using the observation log to review class sessions.

Counter-metric: Abandonment after low-confidence results. If users are leaving the product at uncertain outputs rather than retaking or saving as unidentified, the low-confidence treatment is too alarming rather than helpfully honest.

10 Product tradeoffs

Simplicity vs. accuracy. Confidence bands simplify a continuous score into three buckets. A 68% and an 89% both read as "high confidence." For a research application where data quality matters, that flattening is a deliberate tradeoff: expert users can see the underlying score (kept visible but de-emphasized), while novice users are guided by the band label. The tradeoff is clarity over granularity.

Automation vs. human judgment. The AI can identify with high confidence, but it cannot know whether a student's observation context matters. Keeping human override always accessible (via candidate selection, retake, or save-as-unidentified) means the product never forces false precision on the user's behalf.

Speed vs. credibility. Fast capture matters in field conditions. But a fast capture of a blurry image produces a low-confidence prediction that still requires the user to make a decision. The retake prompt adds a step but protects data quality for the use cases where the observation is actually being used scientifically.

Novice guidance vs. expert control. Students need clear direction. Field researchers need access to metadata, exportable notes, and model score detail. The layered confidence display (band label for novices, raw score visible for those who want it) is the same tradeoff applied consistently across the product.

11 What I'd validate next

The current state has early prototype validation, but not full field validation. Before moving to higher-fidelity design, I'd run three additional rounds of moderated usability research:

  • Field condition usability: recruit 6–8 students in actual classroom field-day contexts, observe capture behavior with wet hands and direct sunlight, measure task completion and error recovery rather than satisfaction
  • Trust calibration: A/B test the high-confidence vs low-confidence result variants with 12+ student participants, measure whether inverted action hierarchy actually shifts behavior toward retake when confidence is low (vs. the rate of save-anyway clicks as counter-signal)
  • Teacher review workflow: semi-structured interviews with 5–6 teachers about classroom adoption barriers, including a think-aloud protocol on the observation review and export flow

Each round would feed back into the design hypotheses. The point of building the technical infrastructure first was to make this research possible at all. Without working ML, the trust questions can't be tested honestly.

12 Reflection

The confidence signal problem turned out to be the more interesting design challenge. Translating a model output into an actionable interface decision is something that comes up anywhere ML is surfaced to a non-technical user. The instinct to display the raw score is almost always wrong. What matters to the user is not the number; it's the answer to "should I trust this and what do I do next."

The three-tier band approach had a tradeoff: it hid granularity in exchange for clarity. A 68% and an 89% both read as "high confidence" in this system. For a research app where data quality matters, that flattening could be a problem, which is why the underlying score was kept visible, just de-emphasized. Expert users can find it. Novice users are guided by the band.

Adding radio selection to the candidate cards was the cleanest change in the project. The problem surfaced in under ten minutes of observation and the fix took an afternoon. That ratio is why I treat early prototype testing as non-optional rather than as a nice-to-have.

13 Product analyst takeaway

This project shows that AI product requirements cannot be written from the model output alone. The workflow around the prediction matters just as much as the prediction itself. The product has to support input quality, confidence interpretation, human correction, and downstream use.

The radio selection gap, discovered in ten minutes of prototype testing, is a good example of why requirements definition needs real users. The written requirement said "show two candidates." What users actually needed was "let me select between them." Those are different requirements with different implementation implications. Only observation revealed the gap.
