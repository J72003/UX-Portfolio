Matching SMB customers to the right digital banking platform

The product decision: which Frost digital banking experience fits each small-business workflow — and how can the product help customers reach that decision before they end up in the wrong tool?

I analyzed SMB platform fit at Frost Bank using competitive benchmarking, stakeholder discovery, and behavior-based research, then translated the findings into a prioritized product direction and a measurement framework. The core analytical move: reframing the question from "which features do SMBs want?" to "which workflow does this business actually run?" — two very different questions with very different product answers.

01 — The problem

The problem — and the cost of getting it wrong

Frost serves business customers through three digital experiences: Online Banking, the mobile app, and Business Connect (a paid platform for operationally complex businesses). "SMB" is not one audience — a two-person consulting firm, a growing eCommerce shop with a payroll cycle, and a regional contractor with approval chains all qualify, and they need completely different things from a bank.

The failure mode was specific and costly: when platform fit is unclear, the safest-feeling choice is the most powerful platform. That default produces underuse, complexity overhead, and churn risk — customers pay for capability they don't need, or land in a tool that doesn't do what they require, and then call a banker to untangle it. Every mismatch also quietly loads cost onto the frontline bankers who absorb the explaining.

How might we help SMB customers — and the bankers supporting them — understand which Frost digital tools fit different business workflows, before they commit to the wrong one?
02 — Analysis

How I analyzed it

The work ran in overlapping phases — research kept feeding back into framing as stakeholder conversations reshaped which questions mattered.

  • Competitive benchmark across 13+ banks — national (Chase, BofA, Wells Fargo, PNC, U.S. Bank), regional, and digital-first (Mercury, Bluevine) — scored across the categories that matter to SMBs: cash-flow forecasting, payment acceptance, invoicing, accounting integrations, lending, pricing transparency, onboarding, and more. Supplemented with external priority data (J.D. Power, BAI, NerdWallet) to anchor against what SMBs industry-wide actually value.
  • Gap analysis across Frost's three platforms to map where capabilities overlap, diverge, and leave customers paying for complexity or missing what they need.
  • Stakeholder discovery with relationship managers, community bankers, treasury contacts, designers, and product owners — the highest-signal research, because frontline bankers hear the recurring customer questions first.
  • Behavior-based interview design — questions built to surface real workflow ("walk me through the last time you sent a payment over $10,000") instead of feature opinions ("would you use dual approval?").
  • Evidence synthesis with a UX manager — interview signals, stakeholder notes, and benchmarking data clustered into themes and translated into prioritized product recommendations.
03 — Key insight

The key insight: workflow predicts fit better than size

The central finding, and the one that moved the team:

Business size did not explain what customers needed. Workflow did. Payment volume, approval structure, user-access requirements, and reporting depth were far stronger signals of platform fit than revenue or headcount. A two-person firm running payroll had heavier needs than an eight-person retail shop.

Three supporting insights, each tied to a product decision:

1. Feature lists create decision friction. Customers don't map "ACH origination" or "dual approval" to their day-to-day work.

→ Decision: use task-based explanations anchored to SMB jobs-to-be-done, not product-first comparison tables.

2. Bankers are part of the product experience. Relationship managers already translate platform fit in conversation, hearing the same three questions every week — early signals of unsolved product problems.

→ Decision: build banker handoff into the experience with context preserved, not as a fallback.

3. Complexity can't be removed, only clarified. Permissions, approvals, and fraud controls are why customers trust the product.

→ Decision: make complexity understandable, not invisible.

04 — Recommendations

Recommendations (MoSCoW-prioritized)

Three recommendations ordered by priority, derived directly from the analysis.

Must Have

Workflow-based onboarding

Replace business-type questions with a short set of workflow questions (payments, approvals, user access), route the customer to a matching platform with a plain-language rationale, and hand off to a banker when inputs are ambiguous rather than dropping them.

First because platform mismatch is the root cause of most conversion and retention friction — no downstream fix matters if customers start in the wrong tool.

Should Have

Task-based navigation

Reframe navigation around what the business is trying to do (pay vendors, manage access, set savings goals) instead of internal product names. Customers do not think in product categories; the product should not require them to.

High impact, more design work; can phase in without blocking onboarding.

Should Have

Banker handoff with context

Surface a clear escalation path at every high-ambiguity decision point, pre-populated with what the customer already provided. Customers who need a human should not have to start over. Escalation is a feature, not a fallback.

Straightforward once routing logic exists; lower volume than onboarding and navigation problems.

Artifact 01 — Current-state journey map · Portfolio concept

The current journey for a new SMB customer choosing a digital banking tool roughly follows this arc: customer has a business banking need, learns there are multiple digital options, is unsure which platform fits, chooses (or is guided into) a platform, encounters friction or unnecessary complexity, and contacts a banker for support.

Pain points sit along the middle of that journey:

  • Platform differences are hard to compare from the customer's vantage point
  • The "right" choice depends on workflow detail the customer may not surface unprompted
  • Paid versus free isn't an obvious decision without context
  • Bankers absorb the burden of explaining options manually
  • Customers can end up on a platform mismatched to their actual needs

Artifact 07 — Future-state user flow · Portfolio concept

The research direction translated into a concept flow for stakeholder discussion. This wasn't a design deliverable. It was a conversation aid for the next phase of work.

A guided experience could follow this shape:

Start → Customer answers a short set of workflow questions → System identifies likely needs → A recommended platform is shown with reasoning → Customer compares features against alternatives → Customer either starts setup or is handed off to a banker

Sample questions, written to surface workflow rather than ask the customer to self-categorize:

  • How many people in your business need access to banking?
  • Do payments in your business require approval from someone else before they go out?
  • How often do you send payments to vendors or contractors?
  • Do you need detailed reporting for accounting or tax purposes?
  • Do you mostly bank from your phone, or from a desktop?
06 — Quantitative signal

Saving Goals as a deposit-activation lever

Beyond the platform-fit scope, I asked to support research on Saving Goals — designing survey questions, administering a 50-participant study, and synthesizing findings with a UX manager.

50 5,000+ 21%+ −7%
study participants active Saving Goals customers monthly-deposit increase after feature interaction monthly deposits for non-adopters, same period

The pattern: goal-based saving behaves like a deposit-activation lever, not a secondary feature. Saving Goals customers make more deposits and larger deposits than comparable non-adopters over the same period.

Product implication: surface Saving Goals activation in onboarding, not six months after account open, where it misses the window that changes behavior.

These are observed product metrics correlated with adoption over a comparable period. They reflect product behavior, not a claim that this research alone caused the lift. I supported rather than owned this work.

07 — Measurement

How I'd measure success

If these recommendations moved into production, I would track the following:

North star metric Platform-fit activation rate: the percentage of new SMB customers who complete onboarding and activate features mapped to their stated workflow within 30 days of account open.

Supporting KPIs:

  • Onboarding completion rate: percentage of customers who complete the workflow-based onboarding flow without abandonment or banker escalation
  • Feature activation breadth: number of platform features activated within 60 days, segmented by business type and complexity
  • Banker escalation rate: percentage of onboarding flows that route to a banker, and whether that rate decreases as the workflow questions improve
  • Saving Goals activation timing: days between account open and first Saving Goal created, comparing customers who encounter it in onboarding versus those who discover it later

Counter-metric: Banker workload per new SMB account. If escalation rates drop but banker workload stays flat or grows, the handoff design needs refinement. Customers may be hitting the banker path for the wrong reasons.

08 — What I'd do differently

What I'd do differently

Interview sample breadth. My direct interviews skewed toward owners already familiar with digital banking. Including customers who'd never used online business banking — or had bad prior experiences — would have sharpened the friction findings.

Confidence ratings per insight. I synthesized themes but didn't systematically rate each by evidence strength before presenting. Some findings were well-supported across sources; others were directional signals from one or two data points. Making that explicit in the readout would have helped stakeholders prioritize and shown the team where validation was still needed before acting.

09 — Product analyst takeaway

Product analyst takeaway

This is how I approach product analysis: start from the real customer workflow, gather evidence from customers and internal stakeholders, benchmark the market, synthesize the patterns, and translate them into prioritized recommendations and a measurement plan.

The strongest insight here wasn't a screen — it was a reframe: SMB platform fit is a workflow-matching problem, not a feature-matching one. The product can't answer "which tool is right for you?" without first knowing how the business moves money. Shifting the team to that question is where the value was.
