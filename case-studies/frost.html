01 — Context

Context

Frost supports business customers through several digital banking experiences, including Frost Online Banking, the Frost mobile app, and Frost Business Connect, a more advanced paid platform built for businesses with greater operational complexity.

During my internship on the SMB portfolio, I focused on a question that sits underneath the surface of "design better screens":

Which Frost digital banking experience actually fits each small or medium business. How does a customer find that out?

Small and medium businesses are not a single audience. A two-person consulting firm, a growing eCommerce shop with three employees and a payroll cycle, and a regional contractor with multiple users and approval chains all fall under "SMB," but they need very different things from a bank. Some need the depth of Business Connect. Others would be better served by simpler free tools. Most don't know which is which until they're already inside the wrong one.

That's a UX problem before it's a product problem.

02 — Problem

The problem

The challenge wasn't that Frost's screens were broken. It was that platform fit was unclear for both customers and the bankers helping them choose.

Three things made this hard:

  1. Differences between platforms aren't easy to see at a glance. Feature lists exist, but a business owner looking at them can't always map "ACH origination" or "dual approval" to whether their business actually needs those things.
  2. Business needs depend on workflow, not just size. Two businesses with the same revenue and headcount can have completely different banking needs depending on how money moves through them.
  3. Internal bankers carry a lot of the customer experience. Relationship managers and community bankers often translate the platform options for customers in conversation. That's a hidden but critical part of the UX.

I framed the problem as a How Might We:

How might we help SMB customers (and the bankers supporting them) understand which Frost digital banking tools fit different business workflows?

04 — Methodology

Methodology

I worked in five overlapping phases. The work wasn't strictly linear. Research kept feeding back into framing, and stakeholder conversations kept reshaping which questions mattered most.

Research & discovery

I started by understanding what "SMB" actually means at Frost: how the bank defines small versus medium business, how those segments differ from retail, and which digital experiences are designed for them today. This gave me a baseline for everything that followed.

Competitive benchmarking

I built a comparison across 13+ banks, including national players (Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, PNC, U.S. Bank), regional banks, and digital-first competitors (Mercury, Bluevine). I compared them across categories that matter to SMBs: digital experience, cash flow forecasting, merchant services, payment acceptance, invoicing, bill pay, accounting integrations, POS and eCommerce integrations, lending, pricing transparency, mobile-first experience, and onboarding.

I supplemented the feature comparison with external customer-priority data from sources like J.D. Power, BAI, and NerdWallet to understand what SMBs across the industry actually value most.

Gap analysis

With the competitive picture in place, I turned inward and ran a gap analysis across Frost Online Banking, mobile, and Business Connect. The goal wasn't to grade the platforms. It was to understand where capabilities overlap, where they diverge, and where a customer might end up paying for complexity they don't need or missing capabilities they do.

Stakeholder discovery

I met with relationship managers, community bankers, treasury management contacts, UX designers, and product owners. These conversations were some of the most useful research I did, because frontline bankers hear the same customer questions repeatedly. Those questions are early signals of UX problems the digital experience hasn't solved yet.

Customer interview planning

I drafted interview questions designed to surface real workflow behavior rather than feature opinions. Instead of "Would you use a dual-approval feature?", the questions moved toward things like "Walk me through the last time you sent a payment over $10,000." Questions like that uncover how the work actually gets done, where it breaks down, and what trust and control mean in that moment.

Design translation (for this portfolio)

For the artifacts shown below, I took a focused slice of the research and translated it into a concept direction: a guided platform-fit experience. These are not Frost-shipped designs. They're my way of showing how the research could inform a UX outcome.

05 — What I learned

Five insights

1. SMB customers are not one-size-fits-all.

Business size is a poor predictor of banking needs. Workflow complexity (number of users, approval requirements, payment volume, reporting depth) predicts platform fit much better than headcount or revenue.

2. Complexity creates decision friction.

When a customer can't tell which tool fits them, the safest-feeling choice is often the most powerful one. That's how customers end up on platforms heavier than they need, which leads to underuse, frustration, and eventually churn risk.

3. Competitors simplify around jobs-to-be-done.

The strongest competitor experiences I benchmarked don't lead with feature lists. They lead with tasks: "send invoices," "manage cash flow," "pay employees." That framing makes the right product feel obvious because it's anchored to what the customer is trying to do.

4. Internal teams are part of the user experience.

Bankers do real UX work in conversation with customers. Any improvement to the digital experience has to either support those conversations or replace them, but never ignore them.

5. Banking UX must simplify without removing necessary complexity.

Permissions, approvals, fraud controls, and compliance aren't friction to be designed away. They're the product. The challenge is making them feel simple, not making them disappear.

06 — Design direction

Design direction

Based on those insights, I explored a single concept direction: a guided platform-fit experience that helps SMB customers and the bankers working with them to understand which Frost digital tools match their business needs.

Three ideas inside that direction:

  • Platform-Fit Guidance: a short, workflow-based set of questions that recommends a starting point
  • Task-Based Navigation: framing options around what the business is trying to do, not which product they map to internally
  • Clear Feature Comparison: a side-by-side that respects how customers actually evaluate options, with a clear path to a banker when the decision needs a human

Artifact 01 — Current-state journey map · Portfolio concept

The current journey for a new SMB customer choosing a digital banking tool roughly follows this arc: customer has a business banking need, learns there are multiple digital options, is unsure which platform fits, chooses (or is guided into) a platform, encounters friction or unnecessary complexity, and contacts a banker for support.

Pain points sit along the middle of that journey:

  • Platform differences are hard to compare from the customer's vantage point
  • The "right" choice depends on workflow detail the customer may not surface unprompted
  • Paid versus free isn't an obvious decision without context
  • Bankers absorb the burden of explaining options manually
  • Customers can end up on a platform mismatched to their actual needs

Artifact 07 — Future-state user flow · Portfolio concept

The research direction translated into a concept flow for stakeholder discussion. This wasn't a design deliverable. It was a conversation aid for the next phase of work.

A guided experience could follow this shape:

Start → Customer answers a short set of workflow questions → System identifies likely needs → A recommended platform is shown with reasoning → Customer compares features against alternatives → Customer either starts setup or is handed off to a banker

Sample questions, written to surface workflow rather than ask the customer to self-categorize:

  • How many people in your business need access to banking?
  • Do payments in your business require approval from someone else before they go out?
  • How often do you send payments to vendors or contractors?
  • Do you need detailed reporting for accounting or tax purposes?
  • Do you mostly bank from your phone, or from a desktop?
Additional research contribution

Saving Goals

Saving Goals is a purpose-driven savings experience that helps business customers set aside money for what matters most: building an emergency fund, planning a major purchase, or saving toward a milestone. Customers create personalized goals, track progress, celebrate achievements, and build momentum through consistent saving habits.

Outside my initial SMB platform-fit scope, I asked for additional work and supported research on this feature. I designed survey questions, administered a 50-participant UserTesting study, and synthesized findings with a UX manager to identify common business customer savings behaviors, themes, and product opportunities.

50 UserTesting participants
5K+ Active Saving Goals customers
21%+ Increase in monthly deposits after feature interaction
7% Less monthly deposits for non-adopters in the same period

These are observed product metrics connected to the Saving Goals feature after adoption and interaction. Goal-based saving correlated with increased deposits and continued engagement. Saving Goals customers made more and larger deposits than non-Saving Goals customers in a comparable period. These figures reflect product behavior, not a claim that this research alone caused the lift.

09 — Reflection

Reflection

The biggest thing this project taught me is that UX in banking is rarely about cleaner screens. It's about helping customers make better decisions inside a system that has real complexity: permissions, security, business rules, regulatory constraints. That stuff can't and shouldn't be designed away.

What I had to learn was how to hold all of that at once: the customer's workflow, the banker's context, the competitive landscape, the platform's actual capabilities, and the business reasoning behind why things are the way they are. Research doesn't get useful until those pieces line up.

The other thing I took away: the strongest design directions don't come from the prettiest screens. They come from research insights specific enough that a design opportunity becomes obvious. When the insight is sharp, the design direction almost writes itself. When the insight is fuzzy, no amount of polish on the screen will fix it.

If I were continuing this work, the next steps would be running the customer interviews against the question set I drafted, validating the platform-fit concept through usability testing with real SMB owners and bankers, and partnering with engineering to understand what's feasible inside Frost's platform architecture before moving to higher-fidelity design.
